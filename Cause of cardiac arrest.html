•Coronary Artery Disease (CAD): The most common cause of cardiac arrest is underlying coronary artery disease, which is the narrowing or blockage of the coronary arteries that supply blood to the heart muscle. CAD can lead to a heart attack, which can trigger arrhythmias.

•Heart Attack: A heart attack occurs when there is a sudden blockage of blood flow to a part of the heart muscle. It can cause damage to the heart and disrupt the heart's electrical system, leading to arrhythmias and cardiac arrest.

•Heart Failure: Chronic heart failure, a condition in which the heart's pumping ability is impaired, can increase the risk of arrhythmias and cardiac arrest.

•Cardiomyopathy: Cardiomyopathy is a disease that affects the heart muscle, making it harder for the heart to pump blood effectively, leading to arrhythmias.

•Electrolyte Imbalances: Abnormal levels of certain minerals (electrolytes) in the blood, such as potassium, calcium, and magnesium, can disrupt the heart's electrical signals and contribute to arrhythmias.

•Drug Abuse: Illicit drug use, particularly stimulants like cocaine and amphetamines, can cause arrhythmias and increase the risk of cardiac arrest.

•Certain Medications: Some medications, especially those that affect the heart's electrical conduction, may increase the risk of arrhythmias.

•Inherited Heart Conditions: Some individuals may have a genetic predisposition to certain heart conditions that can lead to arrhythmias and cardiac arrest.

•Severe Blood Loss or Shock: Significant blood loss or shock can disrupt the heart's electrical system and lead to cardiac arrest.

•Electrical Shock: Exposure to high-voltage electrical currents can cause disturbances in the heart's electrical activity and lead to cardiac arrest.
